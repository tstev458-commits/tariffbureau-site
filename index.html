What Importers

An IEEPA tariff refund is a reimbursement of duties paid under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act. Recent federal court rulings have created legal pathways for U.S. importers to recover duties paid on eligible import entries across multiple HTS classifications from countries including China, Vietnam, Taiwan, and South Korea.

The CBP CAPE portal is the official U.S. Customs filing channel for eligible IEEPA tariff refund claims, launching April 20, 2026. Importers must file through this portal to recover duties paid under IEEPA-based tariff orders. Proper preparation and documentation before filing is critical to maximizing recovery.

U.S. importers who paid IEEPA-related duties on eligible entries from China, Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, India, Bangladesh, Indonesia, or other affected origins may qualify. Foreign manufacturers operating as Importer of Record under DDP terms are also eligible. Eligibility depends on HTS codes, entry dates, and the specific tariff orders applicable to each shipment.

The Tariff Bureau offers multiple service tiers. A preliminary Assessment costs $2,500 flat. A Refund Claim Valuation Report costs $5,000 flat. The Full Recovery Package operates on a contingency model — 25% of the refund recovered, plus a $1,500 engagement retainer credited at recovery. If no refund is recovered, no contingency fee is owed.

Data intake and preliminary eligibility review takes 5 to 7 business days. HTS code analysis and valuation report takes 7 to 10 business days. Claim package preparation and CBP CAPE portal filing takes an additional 5 to 7 business days. Post-filing monitoring continues through recovery, which varies based on CBP processing timelines.

Most U.S. importers are unaware that the CBP CAPE portal exists or that court orders have created refund eligibility. The filing process requires detailed import entry analysis, HTS code expertise, and familiarity with CBP administrative procedures. CBP data reported by Reuters (April 14, 2026) indicates that as of April 9, 2026, approximately 56,497 importers had completed setup covering $127 billion in entries — representing a fraction of the estimated 300,000+ affected importers. Without ACE portal registration and ACH enrollment, CBP cannot issue payment regardless of claim eligibility. The majority of eligible importers have not yet completed the required steps to receive electronic refunds.

May 4, 2026 is the government's appeal deadline on the Court of International Trade order — it is not a universal filing deadline for importers. Protest timing for liquidated entries is entry-specific and is generally tied to each entry's liquidation date and the applicable 180-day protest window after liquidation. Importers with liquidated entries should consult with licensed customs counsel to determine the specific protest deadlines applicable to their entries. We recommend engaging qualified trade counsel promptly given the evolving legal landscape.

Phase 1 of the CBP CAPE process excludes certain entry types including: entries flagged for reconciliation, entries subject to drawback claims, entries subject to antidumping or countervailing duties pending liquidation, entries where liquidation is final or more than 80 days have elapsed since liquidation, and certain duty deferral and temporary importation bond entries. These entry types may be addressed in future phases of the CAPE process. A thorough eligibility assessment is essential to identify which of your entries qualify for Phase 1 recovery.

CBP has stated it will process refund requests within 45 to 90 days from the date of acceptance of a CAPE Declaration, absent any compliance concerns. Refunds are issued exclusively via ACH electronic transfer — paper checks are no longer available as of February 6, 2026. Importers must have completed ACH enrollment in the ACE portal before any refund can be issued. Errors in the CAPE Declaration filing, missing ACH enrollment, or compliance concerns can delay or reject payment entirely.
